Gap-K%: Measuring Top-1 Prediction Gap for Detecting Pretraining Data

Minseo Kwak, Jaehyung Kim
Yonsei University
Paper Code

Gap-K% detects pretraining data by measuring the top-1 prediction gap induced by next-token optimization dynamics, and aggregating locally smoothed gaps across sequences.

Abstract

The opacity of massive pretraining corpora in Large Language Models (LLMs) raises significant privacy and copyright concerns, making pretraining data detection a critical challenge. Existing state-of-the-art methods typically rely on token likelihoods, yet they often overlook the divergence from the model's top-1 prediction and local correlation between adjacent tokens. In this work, we propose Gap-K\%, a novel pretraining data detection method grounded in the optimization dynamics of LLM pretraining. By analyzing the next-token prediction objective, we observe that discrepancies between the model’s top-1 prediction and the target token induce strong gradient signals, which are explicitly penalized during training. Motivated by this, Gap-K\% leverages the log probability gap between the top-1 predicted token and the target token, incorporating a sliding window strategy to capture local correlations and mitigate token-level fluctuations. Extensive experiments on the WikiMIA and MIMIR benchmarks demonstrate that Gap-K\% achieves state-of-the-art performance, consistently outperforming prior baselines across various model sizes and input lengths

Method

Given a token sequence \(x = (x_1,\dots,x_T)\), we compute a token-level gap score, smooth it to reduce noise and capture local patterns, then aggregate the lowest \(K\%\) windows.

1) Token-level Top-1 Prediction Gap

Let \(p(\cdot \mid x_{<t})\) be the next-token distribution at position \(t\). Define:

$$g_t = \frac{\log p(x_t \mid x_{<t}) - \max_{v \in \mathcal{V}} \log p(v \mid x_{<t})}{\sigma_t}$$

where \(\sigma_t\) is the standard deviation of \(\{\log p(v \mid x_{<t}) : v \in \mathcal{V}\}\). Note that \(g_t \le 0\), and values closer to 0 indicate the target token aligns with the model’s top-1 prediction.

2) Sequential Smoothing

To capture local correlations and reduce token-level fluctuation, we compute a windowed average:

$$\bar{g}^{(w)}_t = \frac{1}{w}\sum_{i=0}^{w-1} g_{t+i}$$

where \(w\) is the window size.

3) Gap-K% Aggregation

We aggregate by taking the average of the lowest \(K\%\) values among all windowed scores \(\{\bar{g}^{(w)}_t\}_{t=1}^{T-w+1}\):

$$\mathrm{Gap\text{-}K\%}(x) = \mathrm{Avg}\Big(\mathrm{Bottom}_{K\%}\big(\{\bar{g}^{(w)}_t\}\big)\Big)$$

Interpretation. Scores closer to 0 imply “even the hardest regions align well with the top-1 prediction,” indicating a higher likelihood of membership.

Visualization: Why It Works

  • Min-K%++: tends to pick isolated low-score tokens.
  • Gap-K%: after smoothing, highlights contiguous low-gap segments.

One-line takeaway: Gap-K% detects patterns, not isolated tokens.

Results

Across two benchmarks, Gap-K% demonstrates consistent improvements over strong likelihood-based baselines. On WikiMIA, Gap-K% raises AUROC relative to Min-K%++ across model sizes and input settings, and on MIMIR, it achieves the best average membership inference performance across Pythia variants.
